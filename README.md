# Code Schemes: Radar Modulation and Coding Theory

Repository for embedded DRDO code schemes. This documentation explores the transition from high-power pulsed radar to modern, stealthy, coded signal processing.

---

## 1. Historical Context: The Need for Coding

The development of advanced coding schemes in radar systems was driven by critical physical and operational limitations of simple pulse-echo methods.

### The Problem with High-Power Pulsed Transmission
Initially, radar systems relied on transmitting extremely short, high-power electromagnetic pulses to achieve precise range detection. The range resolution (the ability to distinguish between two close targets) was directly proportional to the shortness of the pulse. However, this approach presented two major challenges:

1.  **Hardware Integrity (Transceiver Overload):** To detect distant targets with a short pulse, the peak power had to be immense. This extreme energy often exceeded physical tolerances, leading to "frying" of sensitive electronics.
2.  **Electronic Intelligence (Stealth):** High-power pulses act as a "lighthouse," allowing enemies to easily detect the radar's location.

### The Shift to Low-Power, Long-Duration Coding
To overcome these barriers, **Pulse Compression** was introduced. Instead of a single high-power burst, the radar sends a longer, lower-power "coded" signal.
*   **Example: LFM (Chirp):** The signal's frequency changes continuously (e.g., low to high).
*   **Matched Filtering:** Upon reception, mathematical filters "compress" the long signal back into a narrow spike, maintaining high resolution without high peak power.

## 2. The 1950s Engineering Challenge: Deterministic Noise

In the mid-20th century, engineers faced a paradox: they needed a signal that was long and low-power, yet "looked" sharp and unpredictable like noise.

### The Requirement: Deterministic Randomness
White noise is perfect for high resolution because it is uncorrelated with anything but itself. However, pure noise is random and unpredictable. The solution was **Pseudo-Random Noise (PRN)**:
*   **Resembled Noise:** Broadband and flat spectrum.
*   **Deterministic:** Generated by a fixed mathematical rule so the receiver could recognize it.

### Hardware Constraints: Vacuum Tubes and Transistors
*   **Vacuum Tubes:** Early logic (flip-flops, XOR gates) was built using bulky, heat-generating vacuum tubes. This limited the complexity of codes due to reliability and power issues.
*   **Transistors:** The maturation of the transistor allowed for **Linear Feedback Shift Registers (LFSRs)**. These could generate **Maximum Length Sequences (m-sequences)** which repeat perfectly every $2^n - 1$ bits while maintaining noise-like properties.

## 3. The Intuition of Pulse Compression

### The "10,000 Syllables" Analogy
Imagine trying to signal someone across a noisy canyon.
*   **High-Power:** You scream a single, incredibly loud "HEY!". It's precise but requires massive peak power.
*   **Coding:** You shout a unique 10,000-syllable poem at a normal volume. The listener "slides" a copy of the poem over what they hear. There is exactly one moment where all 10,000 syllables align perfectly, creating a massive, sharp peak. This is **Auto-correlation**.

### Required Properties of the Code
For a sequence of 1s and 0s to act like a sharp pulse, it must have:
1.  **Balance:** Roughly equal 1s and 0s to ensure a zero DC component and noise-like appearance.
2.  **The "Thumbprint" Property:** If shifted by even one position, the sequence must look completely unrelated to its original version (Low Side-lobes).
3.  **Low Probability of Intercept (LPI):** The signal hides in plain sight, looking like background static to anyone without the "key."

## 5. The Mathematical Foundation: Galois Field $GF(2)$

To build these codes in hardware, engineers use a specific branch of mathematics called **Galois Fields**, specifically $GF(2)$.

### What is $GF(2)$?
$GF(2)$ is a closed mathematical sandbox containing only two numbers: **{0, 1}**. It is the native language of computers because its rules align perfectly with logic gates.

#### The Rules of Arithmetic
In $GF(2)$, we use **Modulo-2 Arithmetic**. Because 2 doesn't exist, we "wrap around" to 0.
1.  **Multiplication (AND Gate):** Works like standard math ($1 \times 1 = 1$; anything else is $0$).
2.  **Addition (XOR Gate):** Addition tracks parity (odd/even). 
    *   $1 + 1 = 0$ (because 2 is even, it resets to 0).
    *   **The Magic Property:** Since $1 + 1 = 0$, it follows that $1 = -1$. **Addition and subtraction are the same operation.** In hardware, this is performed instantly by an **XOR** gate.

## 6. Galois Polynomials: Math for Bitstrings

A **Galois Polynomial** is an expression like $x^3 + x + 1$ where the coefficients are strictly $0$s or $1$s.

### Why use Polynomials?
It allows us to treat a string of bits (like `1011`) as a single mathematical object. 
*   `1011` $\rightarrow 1x^3 + 0x^2 + 1x^1 + 1x^0 \rightarrow x^3 + x + 1$.

By treating bits as polynomials, we can use advanced algebra (division, remainder, factoring) to design codes that have specific "noise-like" properties. Adding two bitstrings is simply XORing them power-by-power.

---

## 7. LFSRs and Primitive Polynomials

The bridge between this abstract math and radar hardware is the **Linear Feedback Shift Register (LFSR)**.

### The "Primitive" Magic
In the same way that "Prime Numbers" cannot be broken down, some Galois polynomials are **Primitive**. 
*   **Intuition:** A primitive polynomial acts as a "mathematical blender."
*   If you build an LFSR based on a primitive polynomial of degree $n$, the register will cycle through every possible combination of bits ($2^n - 1$) in a pseudo-random order before repeating.

### Relation to Radar
1.  **Maximum Length Sequences (m-sequences):** Radars use primitive polynomials to generate these long, deterministic strings.
2.  **Deterministic Noise:** The resulting sequence looks like random noise to an enemy (LPI), but because it was generated by a fixed Galois polynomial ($x^7 + x^3 + 1$), the radar receiver knows exactly what to expect.
3.  **The XOR Advantage:** Because the math is just XORing bits, these complex codes can be generated at gigahertz speeds using very simple, low-power digital circuits—fulfilling the 1950s requirement for high-speed, hardware-efficient "stealth melodies."
